Simpson's Paradox

Simpson's paradox is one of the most surprising — and most important — ideas in statistics. It describes a situation where a trend that shows up in every subgroup of the data reverses once those subgroups are combined. The same numbers can support two completely opposite conclusions, depending only on whether you look at the groups separately or together.

A motivating example

Imagine we collect data on two things for a group of people: how much they exercise each week, and their cholesterol level. We plot exercise on the horizontal axis and cholesterol on the vertical axis. Looking at all the points at once, a clear pattern emerges: people who exercise more seem to have higher cholesterol. The overall trend line slopes upward.

That conclusion feels wrong — and it is. The catch is that we ignored a hidden variable: age.

What's really going on

When we color the points by age group, the picture flips. Within the younger group, more exercise goes with lower cholesterol. The same is true within the middle-aged group, and within the older group. Every single subgroup shows a downward trend: more exercise, lower cholesterol.

So why did the combined data slope upward? Because age is correlated with both variables. Older people tend to have higher cholesterol and, in this dataset, tend to exercise more. When all the groups are stacked together, the position of each group — older groups sitting up and to the right — dominates the overall pattern and hides the real, downward relationship inside each group.

The general lesson

The paradox happens whenever a lurking variable (here, age) splits the data into groups that differ in their baseline levels. Aggregating across those groups can produce a trend that contradicts what is true inside every group. The math isn't broken — both the within-group and the combined calculations are correct. What changes is the question being answered.

That is why the resolution always comes back to one question: which grouping is the right one to act on? Usually, you want to compare like with like — hold the lurking variable fixed — which means trusting the within-group trend. To recommend exercise based on the upward combined line would be exactly backwards.

A famous real-world case

The most cited example comes from UC Berkeley graduate admissions in 1973. Overall, men were admitted at a higher rate than women, which looked like bias against women. But when admissions were broken down by department, women were admitted at an equal or higher rate in most departments. The catch: women applied disproportionately to highly competitive departments with low acceptance rates for everyone, while men applied more to departments that admitted a larger share of applicants. The department was the lurking variable, and once you accounted for it, the apparent bias reversed.

How to protect yourself

  • Look for hidden groups. Before trusting an aggregate trend, ask what subgroups the data contains and whether they differ systematically.
  • Compare like with like. The within-group comparison usually answers the causal question you actually care about.
  • Visualize, don't just summarize. A single correlation number can hide a reversal that a colored scatter plot makes obvious instantly.

Simpson's paradox is a reminder that data never speaks entirely for itself. The way we slice it shapes the story it tells — so the most important step in any analysis is choosing the right way to group.
